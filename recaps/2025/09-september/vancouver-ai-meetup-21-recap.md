## Executive Summary

Vancouver AI Meetup #21 was a watershed moment for the community, centering indigenous knowledge and ceremony alongside cutting-edge AI discussions. ## Key Highlights

### Groundbreaking Keynote: AI + Indigenous Knowledge + Plant Communication

**Manuel Axelstrain** (two-spirit Coast Salish artist, Vines Art Society co-director) delivered the most philosophically profound keynote in Vancouver AI history, presenting an ambitious vision: **using AI to help humans understand what trees and plants are communicating**.

**The Vision:**
- Create AI systems that can interpret the multiple ways plants communicate (mycelium networks, chemical signals, electrical pulses, etc.)
- Combine indigenous knowledge (singing to plants, dream communication, ceremonial harvest) with modern science
- Use this understanding to help regenerate forests and ecosystems
- Center Coast Salish values of honesty, respect, and interconnectedness

**The Paradox:**
Indigenous knowledge systems ask us to **go back** (remember who we are, where we come from). AI asks us to **go forward** (to places we haven't been). Manny's work exists in the tension between these two directions.

**Key Quote:**
> "If indigenous people know that if you sing to the plants they'll grow better, then we can have a lot of humans in the forest singing together, and that will help regenerate the forest better, which can help all of us."

**Art Exhibition Integration:**
Manny's solo show at Richmond Art Gallery uses AI to visualize medicinal compounds in indigenous plants (Paclitaxel from yew trees, lithium, mycelium networks), creating pictographic-style images that bridge microscopic science with ancestral artistic traditions.

**Collaboration Request:**

- **Needed:** Partnerships across disciplines (botanists, AI engineers, indigenous knowledge keepers)

### Indigenous Language Preservation Through AI

**jonny williams** (Squamish Nation) opened the event with traditional welcome song and prayer, then shared his transformative AI story:

**The Challenge:**
- Only **5 fully fluent Squamish speakers** remain
- **60-70 new generation speakers** at adolescent/child level (from recent cohorts)
- As cultural advisor, Johnny reviews 200-300 page official community plans for cultural content
- Finding relevant sections was nearly impossible without fluent reading ability

**The Solution:**
- Johnny trained AI on Squamish information and cultural knowledge he possesses
- Now drops 10 PDFs into AI on Monday morning
- AI pulls all paragraphs needing cultural consultation
- Johnny transformed from "document parser" to **actual cultural advisor**

**Current Use:**
Creating campaign materials for Squamish Nation Council election (vote: September 28th)

**Key Insight:**
AI doesn't replace indigenous expertiseâ€”it **amplifies** it, allowing knowledge keepers to focus on the work only they can do.

**Language Context:**
- **Kamala** (Squamish word): "Place of lots of maples" (what we now call Greater Vancouver)
- Squamish naming philosophy: describe what's happening in a place, don't name after people

## Difficult Conversations: Indigenous Data Sovereignty + AI Ethics

### The Core Tension

**Kris KrÃ¼g Lag Carpenter** raised critical indigenous AI ethics question to Manny:

> "One of the hard things as indigenous folks: there's protocol, rules, regulations around how we use and deploy our knowledge (story, song, dance, painting). What are the ethics/protocols you're following when putting work into AI where there's potential for loss of control?"

**Manny's Response:**

**The Dilemma:**
- Indigenous intelligence has been appropriated and misused throughout history
- If knowledge isn't delivered properly â†’ appropriation, misunderstanding, confusion
- **But:** If indigenous people are left out of AI conversations â†’ potentially more harm

**The Process:**
- Consults with elder Shane Point
- Shane's guidance: "We need everyone. Young people need to find a way to make this work"
- Created "Threshold" exhibition (2023) exploring this exact tension

**The Technical Solution:**
- Programmed AI to follow UNDRIP (UN Declaration on Rights of Indigenous Peoples)
- Programmed AI to follow principles of consent
- Result: AI will say "I cannot tell you that" when appropriate

**Quote:**
> "It's quite an easy adjustmentâ€”just ask it to follow UNDRIP and follow consent. And it will just point blank tell you, 'I can't tell you that.'"

### The Intention vs. Communication Problem

**Audience Question:**
"There is a difference between communication and intention. How do you measure intention? Nature is straightforward, but we're not."

**Manny's Profound Response:**

**Traditional Protocol:**
When Coast Salish people welcome visitors, traditionally you approach and **state your intentions**. Hosts then decide: "Okay, sounds good. You're welcome."

**Historical Betrayal:**
When settler people came, they **stated intentions** that weren't their **true intentions**. This is an issue Coast Salish and Interior Salish people have dealt with for 150+ years: **How do we trust your intentions?**

**AI Challenge:**
Cannot know if what the tree/plant AI is saying is real. Trees live 2,000 yearsâ€”we might only capture micro-signals, not full messages.

**Approach:**
Think of plant AI communication as **collection of data samples** we can try to read, rather than direct translation.

**Cultural Solution:**
> "Coast Salish people really value and honor honesty and respect. Intuitively, this is why we have to be the ones that do this, because we value that more than I think a greater society does. We believed people's intentions, and that's why we have all these people from other places here. That's why we have all this beautiful city here, because we just trusted, we took your word for it, and we still are."
